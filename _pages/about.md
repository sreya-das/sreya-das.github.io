My research focuses on quantum optimal control, magnetic resonance, and control theory, with an emphasis on robustness, physical realizability, and system-level understanding of quantum dynamical systems. I currently work on developing robust optimal control pulses for parahydrogen-induced polarization transfer (PHIP) with applications to quantum-enhanced hyperpolarized MRI, where the goal is to design control strategies that remain effective under realistic experimental imperfections which are state-dependent and state-independent. This work forms part of the QuE-MRT project in collaboration with NVISION, where optimized control pulses are being integrated into the POLARIS PHIP polarizer to generate hyperpolarized carbon-13 agents for enhanced MRI imaging. I have experience on theoretical and experimental contributions to chirp and composite pulse design in NMR, explaining the mechanisms of adiabatic excitation and developing robust composite pulse combinations for broadband spin manipulation. In addition, I have worked on classical control theory, proposing a refined method for controllable and observable canonical transformations for efficient pole placement in linear systems. Overall, my work connects quantum spin physics, optimal control, and classical systems theory, with the broader goal of advancing quantum information, sensing, and technologically relevant control applications.
